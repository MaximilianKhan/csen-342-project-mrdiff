===============================================================================
1. Introduction
===============================================================================

This report presents our baseline replication of "Multi-Resolution Diffusion
Models for Time Series Forecasting" (mr-Diff) by Shen, Chen, and Kwok (ICLR
2024). mr-Diff decomposes a forecast signal into S frequency-resolution stages
via average-pooling trend extraction, then trains a separate denoising diffusion
network per stage. At inference, generation proceeds coarse-to-fine: each finer
stage is conditioned on the prediction of the previous coarser stage, and the
final forecast is the sum of all stage outputs.

We implement the core architecture and evaluate on the ETTh1 and ETTm1 datasets
in both univariate and multivariate settings. Our best run achieves MAE of
0.92-1.01 on normalized data versus the paper's 0.15-0.42, a 2-6x gap. Through
five iterative experimental runs we trace this gap primarily to a train-test
conditioning mismatch inherent to cascaded multi-stage diffusion, which is
severely amplified by the absence of DPM-Solver for inference. Notably, we find
that every paper-described component we tested (cumulative trend decomposition,
learned mixup projection, full signal reconstruction via stage summation)
degraded performance when used with standard DDPM sampling, raising questions
about the completeness of the paper's described recipe for replication.

===============================================================================
2. Implementation
===============================================================================

Our model uses S=5 stages, K=100 diffusion steps with a linear variance
schedule (beta_1=1e-4 to beta_K=0.1), hidden dimension 256, and trend
extraction kernels [5, 25, 51, 201]. The conditioning network is a two-layer
MLP (LeakyReLU 0.1) that encodes the lookback window and fuses it with the
coarser stage's output. The denoising network is a convolutional encoder-decoder
with sinusoidal step embeddings. Training uses Adam (lr=1e-3), gradient clipping
at norm 1.0, and early stopping (patience 20, minimum 30 epochs, max 100).

We decompose the forecast using a residual approach: each component is the
difference between consecutive trend levels, so their sum reconstructs the
original signal. Instance normalization (RevIN) is applied per-window; all
metrics are computed on normalized data to match the paper. At inference, 10
trajectories are sampled and averaged via standard DDPM reverse diffusion
(the paper uses DPM-Solver, which we did not implement).

We evaluate on ETTh1 (hourly, 17,420 samples, lookback 336, forecast 168) and
ETTm1 (15-min, 69,680 samples, lookback 1,440, forecast 192), each in
univariate (OT column) and multivariate (all 7 features) settings, with a
60/20/20 chronological train/val/test split.

===============================================================================
4. Analysis
===============================================================================

Through five runs and systematic ablation, we trace the performance gap to a
train-test conditioning mismatch: each stage trains on ground-truth coarser
components but receives noisy predictions at inference, causing cascading
errors. We tested every paper-described component -- cumulative trend
decomposition (MAE 6-32, catastrophic), learned mixup projection (MAE 1.3-3.5,
regression), and full-signal summation (MAE 1.2-6.9, regression despite our
best-ever training losses) -- and found that each degraded performance under
standard DDPM sampling. Our best results use only the finest stage's prediction,
sidestepping cascading errors at the cost of discarding coarser components. This
suggests the paper's results depend critically on DPM-Solver not merely for
inference speed (as presented in Section 5.2) but for sample quality: without
it, stage predictions are too noisy to compose coherently, and the multi-
resolution framework underperforms even a single-stage approach.

===============================================================================
5. Conclusion
===============================================================================

Our baseline replication of mr-Diff achieves MAE of 0.92-1.01, a 2-6x gap from
the paper's 0.15-0.42. Through five runs and systematic ablation, we found that
the dominant cause is cascading error from the train-test conditioning mismatch,
critically amplified by DDPM's inferior sample quality relative to DPM-Solver.
We further found that each paper-described component we tested (cumulative
decomposition, learned mixup projection, full-signal summation) individually
degraded performance in our DDPM-based setting, suggesting that the paper's
architecture requires DPM-Solver not as an optional accelerator but as a
necessary component for correctness. In our final report we will target this
gap with improved inference sampling and conditioning strategies.
